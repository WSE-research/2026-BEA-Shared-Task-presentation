The task: rubric-based ASAG for German

Dataset: ALICE-LP-1.0 — German STEM short answers (Chemistry, Biology, Physics, Math).

Input: question + student answer + a textual rubric.
Output: Correct / Partially correct / Incorrect.

Metric: Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK).
Size: 7,899 train · 5,176 test.

3-way2-way
Seen questionsTrack 1Track 2
Unseen questionsTrack 3Track 4

2-way merges "Partially correct" into "Incorrect".
Unseen questions = 39 entirely new questions with new rubrics — the hardest setting, requiring rubric generalization.

The task is automated short-answer grading in German, but with a twist: each item comes with a textual rubric that defines what counts as correct. We predict one of three labels, and we're scored with quadratic weighted kappa, which rewards getting close on ordinal labels. There are four tracks along two axes: three-way versus two-way scoring, and — crucially — seen versus unseen questions. The unseen-questions tracks give you 39 brand-new questions with new rubrics, so the system has to generalize from understanding rubrics, not from memorizing questions. That's the hard part. [~70s]

Why German rubric-based scoring is hard

  • The rubric is the primary signal. 18 of 78 questions are generic ("Begründe deine Auswahl!") — only the rubric tells you what's correct.
  • Cross-question generalization. The unseen-question tracks need the model to apply new rubrics it never saw in training.
  • "Partially correct" is a fuzzy boundary. The most subjective label; the dominant error is calling Incorrect answers "Partially correct" (too lenient).
  • Length is a confounder. Correct answers average 267 chars vs. 140 for Incorrect — a tempting but dangerous shortcut for German STEM answers that can be legitimately short.
  • A quantitative + qualitative resource gap for German ASAG vs. English.
Four things make this hard. First, the rubric carries the meaning — almost a quarter of the questions are so generic that without the rubric you literally can't grade them. Second, generalization: the unseen-question tracks force you to apply rubrics the model never trained on. Third, "partially correct" is genuinely fuzzy, and models tend to be too lenient. And fourth, answer length correlates with correctness, which is a trap — German STEM answers can be short and still correct. So you can't just count characters. [~60s]

Our multi-strategy pipeline

1 · Prompting

  • Rubric-conditioned
  • Smart few-shot: TF-IDF + boundary examples
  • Adaptive by question difficulty
→

2 · Fine-tuning

  • LoRA on Qwen family
  • 7B → 14B → 32B → 72B
  • Trained on rubric+answer
→

3 · Aggregation

  • QWK-weighted vote
  • Per-track strategy:
  • ensemble = seen Q · 72B solo = unseen Q

Key design choice: different tracks want different systems. An ensemble of comparable models wins on seen questions; a single large fine-tuned model generalizes best on unseen questions.

Our system has three building blocks. Block one is prompting: we condition on the rubric and add a few carefully chosen examples — selected by TF-IDF similarity plus deliberate boundary cases, and we adapt how many examples by how hard the question is. Block two is fine-tuning: LoRA adapters on the Qwen family, scaled from 7 up to 72 billion parameters. Block three is aggregation: a QWK-weighted vote. The key insight is on the right: we don't ship one system. For seen questions, an ensemble of comparable models is best; for unseen questions, a single large fine-tuned model generalizes better — so we pick the strategy per track. [~70s]

  • Few-shot is the single strongest lever — smart example selection adds ~+0.06 QWK over rubric-only.
  • Prompt ↔ model coupling: our German prompt works for Gemini & Qwen3.5; XML/English helps Claude/GPT but hurts Gemini.
  • Gemini 3 Flash is the best single commercial API model — at ~$0.0002 / answer.
On the prompting side, the biggest lever by far is few-shot example selection — going from a rubric-only prompt to smart example selection buys about six QWK points. We pick examples by similarity plus deliberate boundary cases. One subtle finding: prompts and models are tightly coupled. Our German prompt works for Gemini and for Qwen3.5, but an XML, English-style prompt that helps Claude and GPT actually hurts Gemini. The best single commercial API model was Gemini 3 Flash at 0.748 — and it's cheap, about two hundredths of a cent per answer. But — and this is the next slide — open source closes the gap. [~65s]

  • 14B and 32B beat Gemini on unbiased out-of-sample QWK.
  • Generation > size: Qwen3.5-9B (0.756) edges Qwen2.5-14B (0.753).
  • Scaling saturates ~32B for this data size — 72B matches 32B.
  • Fine-tuned models are >99% confident → no confidence gating.
Now fine-tuning. We LoRA-tuned the Qwen family and evaluated out-of-sample, which is the honest estimate. Two open-source models — 14B and 32B — actually beat the commercial Gemini baseline on unbiased evaluation. A nice result: model generation matters more than raw size — Qwen3.5 at 9 billion edges out Qwen2.5 at 14 billion. And scaling saturates: 72 billion matches 32 billion for a dataset of this size, so there's no point going bigger here. One practical note: the fine-tuned models are over 99% confident, so confidence-based re-ranking gave us nothing. The headline: you don't need a proprietary model for competitive German ASAG. [~65s]
Methodological takeaways

Lessons learned

  • Example selection > prompt tricks. Smart few-shot beats elaborate instructions; forcing structured reasoning hurt.
  • Ensembles need comparable members. A weak model in the vote drags QWK down.
  • Scaling saturates. ~32B was the sweet spot; 72B added nothing on this data.
  • Evaluate out-of-sample. In-sample trial QWK (~0.85, all-data) badly overstated true test performance (~0.77) — train on training data only for an honest estimate.
Four methodological takeaways. One: which examples you show matters more than clever prompt wording — and forcing the model to reason step-by-step actually hurt us by making it over-predict "partially correct". Two: ensembles only help when members are comparable; one weak model poisons the vote. Three: scaling saturated around 32 billion parameters. And four — a measurement lesson — evaluate out-of-sample: training on all data and scoring the trial set gave about 0.85, but that's memorization; the honest out-of-sample number was about 0.77, much closer to real test performance. Trust the unbiased estimate. [~55s]

Conclusion & future work

Competitive open-source, rubric-based ASAG for German is feasible — second on three of four tracks, within 0.02 QWK of the best system, with fine-tuned Qwen models that beat a commercial baseline out-of-sample.

  • The frontier is generalization. Track 4 (unseen questions, 2-way) sits at 0.533 — the clear next target.
  • Rubric decomposition & better handling of the "Partially correct" boundary.
  • Planning BEA 2027 early — and involving students for more experimentation bandwidth.
